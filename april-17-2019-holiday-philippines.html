1. April 9th is a public holiday in the philippines to commemorate the fall of bataan during world war ii. this day is also known as:


ᗩᖇᗩᗯ ᑎᘜ KᗩᘜITIᑎᘜᗩᑎ / ᗷᗩTᗩᗩᑎ ᗪᗩY

__________________________

Araw ng Kagitingan commemorates the fall of the Bataan peninsula during the second world war. Called 'Araw ng Kagitingan' in Filipino, this official regular nationwide holiday is celebrated annually on April 9th.

If the date clashes with an Easter holiday, then the day may be moved as was the case in 2009. The holiday may also be known as 'Bataan Day' or 'Bataan and Corregidor Day'. It commemorates the fall of the Bataan peninsula during the second world war.

on 1987, it was renamed officially as 'Araw Ng Kagitingan' (Day of Valor) to pay tribute to those who defended Bataan, Corregidor and Bessang. From 2007, the holiday was designated to be observed on the Monday nearest April 9th, but since 2009 a yearly presidential decree has meant it is observed on April 9th. As it is decreed as an official regular nationwide holiday, employers should abide by the Holiday pay rules.

Based on the data gathered from World population review (Accessed April 3, 2019) on the population of the Philippines in 2019, the top three cities in terms of population in the Philippines are Quezon City with 2, 761, 720; Manila with 1, 600, 000; and Caloocan City with 1, 500, 000. Write the scientific notation of the total population of these three cities.​


Expressing Numbers in Scientific Notation

Quezon City - 2, 761, 720

    Scientific Notation: 2.761720 x [tex]10^{6}[/tex]

Manila - 1, 600, 000

    Scientific Notation: 1.60 x [tex]10^{6}[/tex]

Caloocan City -1,  500, 000

    Scientific Notation: 1.50 x [tex]10^{6}[/tex]

Step-by-step explanation:

Scientific notation is a shorthand method to write very large or very small numbers. Numbers are expressed in terms of exponents of 10 in a scientific notation. To write a number in scientific notation, we follow the steps below.

1. Move the decimal point to the right of the first digit in the number. Take note that if the number is a whole number, the decimal point is after the last digit or number.

  For example, the number is 2, 761, 720, we move the decimal point to the right of the first digit making it 2. 761720.

2. Count the number of places n that you moved the decimal point.

  In the previous example, we moved the decimal point six times.

3. Multiply the decimal number by 10 raised to a power of n. The n here is the number of times we moved the decimal point.

  The number now based on the example is 2.7610 x  [tex]10^{6}[/tex]

4. Indicate the sign of n or the power. If the decimal point was moved to the left as in a very large number, the n is positive. If the decimal point is moved to the right as in a very small number, the n is negative.

   In the previous example, since we move the decimal to the right then n is positive, giving us 2.7610 x  [tex]10^{6}[/tex]
