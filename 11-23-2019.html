Because the government and LGUs based the suspension on the intensity of the typhoon, the different classification of tropical cyclones is related to the suspension of classes.

The public storm warning system (PSWS) is as follows:

PSWS No. 1: The initial signal indicates that rain will fall intermittently for at least 36 hours in the affected area. Winds of 30 to 60 kilometers per hour are forecast, but they are unlikely to cause significant damage. Classes in all public and private pre-schools have been automatically suspended as a precaution.

PSWS No. 2: The second warning signal is raised in locations where winds of 60 kph to 100 kph are expected to occur during the next 24 hours. It's likely that just minor to moderate harm may occur. It's possible that some trees could be uprooted and rooftops will be blown away. Travelers by air and sea are advised to exercise caution, and emergency preparedness authorities should notify their respective communities. Classes from kindergarten to high school have been canceled.

PSWS No. 3: People are recommended to seek shelter within sturdy buildings, leave low-lying regions, and keep away from beaches and riverbanks when the third warning signal is raised, since moderate to serious damage is likely. Within the next 18 hours, winds of 100 kph to 185 kph are expected. Winds have the potential to collapse trees, ruin crops, and destroy dwellings made of light materials. Electrical power and communication services are also predicted to be disrupted on a large scale. Classes are automatically halted at all levels.

PSWS No. 4: The fourth storm warning signal warns that a powerful typhoon with gusts of over 185 kph is possible during the next 12 hours. The storm has the potential to be quite devastating. Large trees are likely to be uprooted, and residential and institutional structures may sustain significant damage. It is recommended that all travel and outdoor activities be cancelled.

PSWS No. 5: When a super typhoon is expected to hit a region, this newly enhanced storm warning signal is activated. In at least 12 hours, very strong winds of more than 220 kph are possible. This typhoon is projected to be "very destructive or catastrophic" to the neighborhood, with practically total destruction of structures. Most residential and institutional structures are likely to be severely damaged, with only a few crops and trees remaining. Evacuation to safer shelters should begin as soon as possible because it may already be too late if it hasn't already started. By this stage, the disaster coordinating councils involved, as well as other disaster response agencies, should be fully operational or ready to respond quickly to the disaster.
