1. anong isyu sa pharmally?​


Answer:

MANILA – President Rodrigo Duterte welcomed calls for the Ombudsman to conduct its own investigation into the controversial multibillion-peso deals between the government and Pharmally Pharmaceutical Corp.

This, after the Philippine College of Physicians (PCP) urged the Ombudsman to file the appropriate cases against all those responsible for any corruption.

“We welcome the call of the Philippine College of Physicians that this matter should immediately be given to our courts and the Ombudsman. We have been saying this for the longest time and we all know that the Senate cannot prosecute. Mabuti pa ang mga doktor, alam at naintindihan nila (At least the doctors understand). Filing cases is the right way to go,” he said in a pre-recorded public address aired late Tuesday night.

Duterte urged the Senate Blue Ribbon Committee, which conducts a series of probes into the Pharmally controversy, to stop using the issue “in aid of election.”

“Problema kasi sa Senate natin, trying hard maghanap ng mga issue na may nangungurakot o kaya may nagkakapera (The problem with the Senate is that it’s trying hard to look for issues of corruption) but up to now the committee has not really built a case out of the numerous hearings they have conducted,” he said.

He said there was no overpricing in the government’s medical supplies procurement, noting that they were consistent with Republic Act (RA) 9184 or the Government Procurement Reform Act and RA 11469 or the Bayanihan to Heal as One Act.

Duterte said the Senate panel is free to file charges against companies involved in tax-evading.

“If you think you have identified companies with tax issues then file the appropriate cases. Matagal naman naming sinasabi: Kasuhan niyo ang mga dapat kasuhan. Lahat naman talaga dapat magbayad ng buwis (we have been saying for the longest time: File charges. Everyone should pay their taxes),” he added.

He challenged the Senate to bring before the Supreme Court his memorandum prohibiting executive officials from attending Senate probes.

PHARMALLY DEAL INQUIRY BY SENATE BLUE RIBBON COMMITTEE

ContextHearings conducted by the Senate's blue ribbon committee revealed that Pharmally secured P10 billion in pandemic contracts between 2020 and 2021 despite the fact that it was a small, recently founded company without the resources, track record, or legitimacy to manage significant government contracts.One of the businesses PS-DBM proposed and selected to provide face masks, test kits, PPEs, and other hospital instruments during the pandemic was Pharmally, the subject of the current Senate probe.

In relation to Article III for Bills of Rights in 1987 ConstitutionNON-IMPAIRMENT OF THE OBLIGATION OF CONTRACTS: The duty of contracts cannot be compromised by the use of the taxing power. Most of the sections in the Article III are applied for the protection of the witnesses. The case of Pharmally and the Past Governance in the Philippines refers to the controversy surrounding the pharmaceutical company Pharmally and its alleged involvement in corruption and unethical practices during the previous administration in the Philippines.

In terms of the 8 principles of Good Governance as per the UNDP notion, the following can be noted in relation to this case:

a. Participation: There was a lack of public participation in the decision-making process surrounding Pharmally and its activities, leading to a lack of transparency and accountability.

b. Rule of Law: The alleged corruption and unethical practices of Pharmally were a violation of the rule of law, which upholds principles of justice and equality.

c. Transparency: The lack of transparency in the dealings of Pharmally with the previous administration undermines public trust and raises questions about the accountability of those involved.

d. Responsiveness: The previous administration's failure to respond to public concerns about Pharmally and its activities suggests a lack of responsiveness to the needs and concerns of the citizens.

e. Consensus Oriented: The decisions surrounding Pharmally and its activities were not made through a consensus-oriented process, leading to a lack of legitimacy in the eyes of the public.

f. Equity and Inclusiveness: The actions of Pharmally and the previous administration may have favored certain individuals and groups over others, violating the principles of equity and inclusiveness.

g. Effectiveness and Efficiency: The alleged corruption and unethical practices of Pharmally and the previous administration may have resulted in inefficiencies and reduced effectiveness in the governance and management of the country.

h. Accountability: The lack of accountability in the dealings of Pharmally and the previous administration undermines the principle of good governance and raises questions about the integrity of those involved.

The noted critiques of Good Governance include the following:

Lack of clarity in definition: There is a lack of consensus on what constitutes Good Governance, leading to confusion and disagreement about its implementation.

Western-centric perspective: The concept of Good Governance has been developed primarily from a Western perspective, leading to criticisms that it is not applicable to other cultural and political contexts.

Emphasis on formal institutions: Good Governance is often seen as being primarily concerned with formal institutions, ignoring the importance of informal networks and relationships.

Presumption of positive outcomes: Good Governance is often seen as a guarantee of positive outcomes, ignoring the complex and often unintended consequences of governance practices.

Top-down approach: The approach to Good Governance is often seen as being imposed from the top-down, neglecting the importance of bottom-up approaches and citizen engagement.

Unawareness of power dynamics: Good Governance often overlooks the role of power dynamics in shaping governance outcomes, leading to an oversimplification of the complexities of governance.

In conclusion, while Good Governance is an important concept, it is important to understand its limitations and to critically evaluate its implementation in different contexts
