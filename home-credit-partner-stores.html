A debit to the income summary ledger account and a credit to the home office account appear in the accounting records of the branch to record the net income of the branch.

If the head office sends money to the branch office, the bookkeeping carried out at the head office is debiting the branch office account, and crediting the cash estimate. The branch office book that receives money from the head office will be recorded on the debit side of the cash account and credit for the head office account. The recording aims to determine the amount of remittances from the head office and vice versa the office must make a record at its branch office, which is remittances, so that it can know the overall amount of money sent to all existing branches.

BRANCH OFFICE ACCOUNTING

Branch Office has the authority to conduct sales transactions. Therefore the Branch Office carries out separate bookkeeping. So both the Head Office and Branch Offices organize separate accounting records. This listing is only useful for internal parties of the Head Office and Branch Offices. For the interests of external parties Head Office prepares a consolidated report namely the financial statements containing the Combined Financial Performance of Head Office and Branch Offices. Unlike the head office investment in the agent's office, which only in the form of initial working capital, investments invested by Head Office to Branch Office covers all initial office needs Branch . Therefore it can be said that the Headquarters acted as Investor (funding party) and Branch Office as Investee (the recipient of funds). Therefore an account is required which is reciprocal (reciprocal) between the Head Office and the Office Branch to accommodate this reciprocal transaction, Office The head office uses the account name of the Branch Office, otherwise the Office Branch using Head Office account. 28. When the fair market value of the assets contributed by an incoming partner exceeds the amount to be credited to his capital account, the difference is bonus to the new partner


If the fair market value of the assets contributed by an incoming partner exceeds the amount to be credited to his capital account, the excess amount is often referred to as a "bonus" to the new partner. This can occur in a partnership when the partnership agreement provides for the allocation of a bonus to a new partner in recognition of the value of the assets that the partner is bringing into the partnership.

In general, the allocation of a bonus to a new partner is a matter of negotiation between the existing partners and the incoming partner. The partners may consider a variety of factors in determining the amount of the bonus, such as the value of the assets being contributed, the relative contributions of the existing partners, and the potential benefits to the partnership as a result of the incoming partner's participation.

It's important to note that the allocation of a bonus to a new partner may have tax implications for both the partnership and the individual partners. Partners should consult with a tax professional to understand the potential tax consequences of any bonus allocation.
