Answer: A lack or oversupply of money can have significant impacts on an economy and its people. Maintaining an optimal level of money supply is crucial for the sustainable economic growth of a country.

Explanation:

Lack of Supply of Money:

When there is a shortage or lack of money supply in an economy, it can lead to several consequences such as:

1. Decrease in Spending: People have less money to spend, which can lead to a decrease in consumer spending, resulting in lower demand for goods and services.

2. Deflation: If the money supply decreases, the value of money increases, which can lead to deflationary pressures. Prices of goods and services may decrease, leading to a fall in overall economic activity.

3. Higher Interest Rates: In a shortage of money, banks and financial institutions have less money to lend, and therefore, they may increase the interest rates, making it harder for businesses and consumers to borrow money.

Oversupply of Money:

When there is an oversupply of money, the effects can also be varied, and some of them include:

1. Inflation: An oversupply of money in the economy can lead to an increase in demand for goods and services, which can lead to an increase in prices, causing inflation.

2. Decreased Value of Currency: As the supply of money increases, the value of currency may decrease, which can lead to a reduction in its purchasing power.

3. Lower Interest Rates: In an oversupply of money, banks and financial institutions may lower their interest rates to stimulate borrowing and investing, as there is a lot of money available for lending.

Note that each downsides have different ripple effect and may not directly affect the "current" situation of the economy. Sometimes, the effects of say for example Lower Interest Rates kick-in later in the near future. I suggest you to research on economics to understand more.

15. bakit nagkakaroon ng oversupply?​


Answer:

In economics, overproduction, oversupply, excess of supply or glut refers to excess of supply over demand of products being offered to the market. This leads to lower prices and/or unsold goods along with the possibility of unemployment.

The demand side equivalent is underconsumption; some consider supply and demand two sides to the same coin – excess supply is only relative to a given demand, and insufficient demand is only relative to a given supply – and thus consider overproduction and underconsumption equivalent.

Overproduction is often attributed as due to previous overinvestment – creation of excess productive capacity, which must then either lie idle (or under capacity), which is unprofitable, or produce an excess supply.
