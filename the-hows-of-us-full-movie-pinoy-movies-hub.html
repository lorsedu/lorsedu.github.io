10. Differentiate Hub, Switch, and Router according to their uses and effect on the network​


Difference of  Hub, Switch, and Router according to their uses and effect on the network

Switches, hubs, and routers are occasionally used interchangeably in networking, which is incorrect.

Despite their similarities, they manage data in different ways. These three components may be combined into a single gadget, making it difficult for a pupil to tell them apart.

What exactly is a switch?

A switch is a multicast networking device that joins a group of computers or devices in a network. It operates at the Datalink layer of the OSI architecture. It's primarily used to send a private message and consumes no data.

By employing a MAC address, a switch can simply determine which device is connected to which port, allowing it to deliver the message to a specific computer.​

The process of moving network traffic from one device connected to one port of a network switch to another device connected to another port is known as forwarding.

What exactly is a hub?

A hub is a low-cost networking device that joins a group of computers in a Local Area Network by operating at the physical layer of the OSI model (LAN). It is seen as less clever because it does not filter data and has no idea where it will be sent.

All data delivered to a hub is automatically relayed to all ports of all linked devices. As a result, bandwidth is squandered.

What exactly is a Router?

A router is a networking device that connects two or more networks by operating at the network layer of the OSI model. It is a device that establishes a common link between networks to enable data flow between.
