2. report about the public storm warning signals of PAGASA​


The Philippine Atmospheric, Geophysical and Astronomical Services Administration (PAGASA) is the government agency responsible for providing weather forecasts and warnings in the Philippines. One of the services that PAGASA provides is the issuance of public storm warning signals.

PAGASA has a standardized warning system that uses a set of signals to inform the public of the level of danger from an approaching storm. The system has a total of 5 warning signals, each one corresponding to a different level of danger from a storm.

The warning signals are:

Signal No. 1: It means that winds of 30-60 kph may be expected within the next 36 hours, the public is advised to be alerted against possible sporadic rains or thunderstorms.

Signal No. 2: It means that winds of 61-120 kph may be expected within the next 24 hours, the public is advised to take precautionary measures and to be alerted for possible landslides, flash floods, and moderate to heavy rain.

Signal No. 3: It means that winds of 121-170 kph may be expected within the next 18 hours, the public is advised to take precautionary measures and to be alerted for possible landslides, flash floods, and heavy to intense rain.

Signal No. 4: It means that winds of 171-220 kph may be expected within the next 12 hours, the public is advised to take precautionary measures and to be alerted for possible landslides, flash floods, and heavy to intense rain.

Signal No. 5: It means that winds of more than 220 kph may be expected within the next 12 hours, the public is advised to take precautionary measures and to be alerted for possible landslides, flash floods, and heavy to intense rain.

PAGASA issues these warning signals through various means such as radio and television broadcasts, SMS, and social media platforms. It is important for the public to take these warning signals seriously and to take the appropriate actions to ensure their safety during a storm.

Answer:

They use 5 only

Answer:

When any Public Storm Warning Signal Number is hoisted or put in effect for the first time, the corresponding meteorological conditions are not yet prevailing over the locality. This is because the purpose of the signal is to warn the impending occurrence of the given meteorological conditions. It must be noted also that the approximate lead time to expect the range of the wind speeds given for each signal number is valid only when the signal number is put in effect for the first time. Thus, the associated meteorological conditions are still expected in at least 36 hours when PSWS #1 is put in effect initially; in at least 24 hours with PSWS #2; in at least 18 hours with PSWS #3, in at least 12 hours with PSWS #4; and in at least 12 hours with PSWS #5. The lead time shortens correspondingly in the subsequent issues of the warning bulletin when the signal number remains in effect as the tropical cyclone comes closer.

23. A weather bulletin was issued by PAGASA that a tropical depression had intensified into a tropical storm. What did this mean? *


Answer:

It means that the maximum sustained surface winds has increased.

Explanation:

A tropical depression is a tropical cyclone that has maximum sustained surface winds (one-minute average) of 38 mph (33 knots) or less while a tropical storm is a tropical cyclone that has maximum sustained surface winds ranging from 39-73 mph (34 to 63 knots). Which means that when a tropical depression intensifies to a tropical storm, its sustained surface wind has increased.
