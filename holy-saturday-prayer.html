Answer:

Here is a sample plan of activities for the Holy Week from Palm Sunday to Black Saturday:

Palm Sunday:

Attend the Palm Sunday mass and receive blessed palm frondsRecreate Jesus' triumphant entry into Jerusalem by joining a procession with palm branchesRead and reflect on the Gospel story of Jesus' entry into Jerusalem

Holy Monday:

Attend mass or a prayer serviceRead and reflect on the story of the cleansing of the templeFast or abstain from meat as a form of penance

Holy Tuesday:

Attend mass or a prayer serviceMeditate on the Parable of the Ten Virgins and reflect on the importance of being prepared for the coming of the LordSpend time in quiet reflection and prayer

Holy Wednesday:

Attend mass or a prayer serviceMeditate on the story of Judas' betrayal of JesusAttend the Tenebrae service, a liturgical service of darkness and lamentation that focuses on the suffering of Jesus

Holy Thursday:

Attend the Mass of the Lord's SupperParticipate in the washing of the feet ritualSpend time in adoration before the Blessed Sacrament

Good Friday:

Attend the Good Friday liturgyVenerate the cross by kissing or touching itMeditate on the Passion of Christ and reflect on the sacrifice that Jesus made for us

Black Saturday:

Attend the Easter Vigil mass in the eveningParticipate in the lighting of the paschal candle and the renewal of baptismal promisesCelebrate the Resurrection of Christ with joy and thanksgiving.

These mysteries represent the significant events in the life of Jesus Christ. The rosary is often used as a guide for other Catholic prayers. These prayers are related to challenges or celebrations in the life of one praying the rosary or on behalf of another for whom the prayers are said.

Explanation:

The Church has used three sets of mysteries for many centuries. In 2002 Pope John Paul II proposed a fourth set of mysteries—the Mysteries of Light, or Luminous Mysteries. 28. which of the following is the sacred tradition?a. Holy bible b. Prayer book c. prayer d. Holy Rosary​


Answer:

Sacred tradition is a theological term used in major Christian traditions, primarily those claiming apostolic succession, such as the Catholic, Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, Assyrian, and Anglican traditions, to refer to the foundation of the doctrinal and spiritual authority of Christianity and of the Bible.

Christians believe that the teachings of Jesus and the Apostles were preserved in the scriptures as well as by word of mouth and were handed on. This perpetual handing on of the tradition is called the "Living Tradition"; it is believed to be the faithful and constant transmission of the teachings of the Apostles from one generation to the next. That "includes everything which contributes towards the sanctity of life and increase in faith of the People of God; and so the Church, in her teaching, life and worship [the Creeds, the Sacraments, the Magisterium, and the Holy Sacrifice of the Mass], perpetuates and hands on to all generations all that she herself is, all that she believes."[1] The Deposit of Faith (Latin: fidei depositum) refers to the entirety of divine revelation. According to Roman Catholic theology, two sources of revelation constitute a single "Deposit of Faith", meaning that the entirety of divine revelation and the Deposit of Faith is transmitted to successive generations in scripture and sacred tradition (through the teaching authority and interpretation of the Church's Magisterium (which consists of the Church's bishops, in union with the Pope, typically proceeding synods and ecumenical councils).
